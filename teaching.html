Teaching

 I am a physics teacher at the High School of American Studies in the Bronx, and have been teaching for nearly 30 years. I also taught at the Bronx High School of Science for many years before that. But my "home", where I began my long career was at the now closed South Bronx High School.

 Building a website for my classes, and later creating animations to help explain the physic concepts I was trying to get across to my students is what initially got me interested in coding. A picture is worth a thousand words, and in physics, a moving picture is sometimes worth even more. Below is a list of some of best animations. These are written in Adobe Flash:

  • Vector Addition
    Move the vector heads around to see how the resultant of the two vectors changes.
  • Adding Several Vectors (Manual Version)
    Connect all of these vectors from head to tail. The resultant is the same no matter what order you use to connect them.
  • Adding Several Vectors (Automatic Version)
    Same idea as the one above, just automatic.
  • Uniform Acceleration
    A wheel rolls down an incline. Try to figure out what the distances will be.
  • Bullet Shot Horizontally
    Bullets actually fall when fired.
  • Monkey Hanging from a Tree.
    Will the hunter hit the Monkey?
  • Billiard Ball Collisions.
    See how momentum affects what will happen when two billiard balls collide (in one dimension).
  • Pulse Motion
    See how a pulse moves and affects the particles over which it moves.
  • Transverse Waves
    See how the particles in a transverse wave move in relation to the motion of the wave.
  • Speaker
    Watch how a speaker produces sound, and how the sound can travel through air.
  • Interference
    See what happens when waves meet up.
  • Standing Waves
    Look at the characteristics of standing waves.
  • Producing Standing Waves
    See how standing waves are produced.
  • Photoelectric Effect
    See the effect light has on metals.
  • The Black Box
    This is the Black Box Lab.
  • Lenses
    See how images are formed by lenses.
